Job Market Paper

Foreign Institutional Investors’ Site Visits and Tunnelling [draft available upon request]

This study investigates whether and how foreign institutional investors’ (FIIs) site visits affect tunnelling through inter-corporate loans. Using all Chinese firms listed in the Shenzhen Stock Exchange from 2012 to 2022, we find that FIIs’ site visits significantly curb tunnelling across three different measures of site visits. By performing propensity score matching and multi-period difference-in-difference analysis, we suggest this relation is causal. This effect is more pronounced when FIIs hold larger shareholdings, as their more intensive and frequent visits complement each other in mitigating tunnelling problems. The curbing effect of FIIs’ site visits on tunnelling is more significant than that of site visits by other institutional investors, including Hong Kong, Macao, and Taiwan (HMT), Sino-foreign, and mainland institutional investors. Finally, we show that FIIs play an effective monitoring role if they visit in groups rather than alone. The study contributes to the literature on tunnelling problems and the disciplining role of FIIs in weak institutional environments.

Working Papers

Foreign Institutional Investors and Underperforming CEOs: Evidence from Chinese Listed Firms (with Martin Strieborny) [draft available upon request]

We examine the potential mechanisms through which foreign institutional investors force out underperforming CEOs. Using a sample of Chinese listed firms from 2009 to 2023, we first show a positive association between foreign institutional shareholdings and CEO turnover-performance sensitivity. We then provide evidence for two transmission channels driving this result — the quality of corporate governance and the stock price informativeness. To mitigate endogeneity concerns, we conduct a staggered difference-in-differences analysis exploiting the exogenous shock to foreign institutional ownership due to the introduction of the Shanghai/Shenzhen–Hong Kong Stock Connect Program.

Do Retail Investors Matter? Investor–Firm Interactions and Corporate Innovation [draft available upon request]

This paper examines whether investor–firm interactions on regulated online platforms affect corporate innovation. Using a sample of 6,115 Chinese A-share listed firms over 2010–2023, we measure both the volume of investor questions and the incremental information disclosed in managerial responses using a novel directional lexical overlap metric. We find that investor–firm interactions are positively and significantly associated with subsequent innovation, measured by granted invention patents. The results are robust to entropy balancing and instrumental variable approaches. Further analysis reveals that financial constraints serve as a partial mediating channel, and the effect is stronger when information asymmetry is higher and institutional monitoring is weaker. Our findings suggest that regulated interactive platforms can promote corporate innovation by reducing information asymmetry between retail investors and firm management.
